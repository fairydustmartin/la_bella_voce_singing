Joaquina Calvo Johnson

Joaquina retired after 25 years from Yuba College in Marysville,
california as a professor of music in 2003. however, she kept a private
voice studio and continued to work as artistic director for both the Yuba Sutter
Master Chorale and the Borgamaria Lyric Opera company.

Joaquina volinteered as president of the Yuba-Sutter Regional Arts Council,
which which she had been involved with since its inception totaling about 25 years.
Through the council, she was involved with the arts education outreach program,
which was very dear to her heart, and the Special Arts program,
which is hosted by the council every spring for children who are
physically and mentally impaired. The council also participates in various
community events, such as the Christmas Stroll and the Harvest Festival.

Joaquina aslo sat on the board of directors for the Tri-County Concert Association,
which she enjoy very much. The group worked very hard to bring internationally acclaimed
artists to the Yuba-Sutter community. Other organizations was involved with were
the American Chorale Directors Association and the National Association of Teachers of Singing.

Joaquina went to see our maker in the year Aug 14, 2012 after a battle with pancreatic cancer.
She was 74 years young. This website is dedicated to her and her knowledge I hope to share
on the Italian style of beautiful singing called la bella voce.

Posture

In humans, posture can provide a significant amount of important information through nonverbal communication.
Psychological studies have also demonstrated the effects of body posture on emotions. The theories that guide
research in this field are the self-validation or perception theory and the embodied emotion theory.
A good singers posture may best be approached by finding a foundation built through a neutral position that
exerts the least amount of energy. body positioning that favors the best functioning of all body parts.
Such positioning balances the distribution of weight, putting the least strain in muscle, tendons,
ligaments and bones.

Breathing

All aerobic creatures need oxygen for cellular respiration, which uses the oxygen to break down foods for energy
and produces carbon dioxide as a waste product. The body's circulatory system transports these gases to and from the ,
cells, where "cellular respiration" takes place. Under normal conditions the breathing depth and rate is automatically,
and unconsciously, controlled by several homeostatic mechanisms which keep the partial pressures of carbon dioxide and
oxygen in the arterial blood constant. During the process of deep breathing with the thoracic diaphragm adopts a lower
position in the core and this helps to generate intra-abdominal pressure which strengthens the lumbar spine. Most people
do not get the nessary amount of oxigen do to breathing that is counter productive. keeping an expanded chest and breathing by
using the diaphram allows for more oxigen to enter the body. the diaphram pushes the lungs up in to the open chest cavity
to empty the co2.

Support

The amount of Vocal intensity is proportional based on the note(s) being sang, the length of time of the note(s),
Phrasing, Volume, and Performance room. Too much is pushing and can cause tension in the vocal track. If you were
to use the same amount of support at the beginging of a phrase that you would at the end, you would be
pushing. The opisite of this is also true. Using the same amount of support you would at the begining at the end wouldn't
be able to suport the sound needed, including consonants.

Phonation

The vocal cords are composed of twin infoldings of 3 distinct tissues: The outer layer is squamous, non-keratinizing epithelium.
Below this is the superficial layer of the lamina propria, a gel like layer, which allows the vocal fold to vibrate and produce
sound. The vocalis and thyroarytenoid muscles make up the deepest portion. These vocal folds are covered with a mucous membrane
and are stretched horizontally, from back to front, across the larynx. The inner lining surface of this squamous epithelium is
covered by a layer of mucus (acting as a mucociliary clearance), which is composed of two layers: a mucinous layer and serous layer.
Both mucus layers provide viscous and watery environment for cilia beating posteriorly and superiorly. The mucociliary clearance
keeps the vocal folds essentially moist and lubricated. Knowing that the falsetto voice is created when the muscle of the vocal folds
are not being used. This knowledge comes in handy when a singer is having issues singing high notes, or singing up to high notes.
There is a percentage of vibration in each part of the vocal folds, the higher you sing, the less of the muscular part is used.

Resonation

Resonation chambers are where the bodies natural areas of amplification takes place. The two main places that resonation takes place are
the head and the thoracic chamber. Head voice is when more resonation happens in the head and chest voice is when more resonation
happens in the chest, singers also have a chest plate and a face plate for amplification. More resonation in the chest is best for
lower voiced singers and more in the head for higher voiced singers. Resonation also happens in the Pharynx, Nasal, oral, Sinus cavities,
amoung other places. Some chambers can be trained to create more space; like an expanded chest with a lowered diaprahm and a lowered jaw
with a forward tongue, a loward larynx and a lifted soft palate. Creating more space in the chest, throat and mouth allowing for a bigger voice. It is importand to remember that
muscles can and do sit over resonating chamber and can effect this.

Vowels

A vowel is a syllabic speech sound that is pronounced without any stricture in the vocal tract.
There are 5 pure vowels that all other vowels are a mix of. These 5 pure vowels that also consist
of no dipthongs (ɛ, e, ɑ, ɔ, u). Each of these 5 pure vowels are so special
to a singers mouth position because it establishes a good mouth embouchure that aids a singer with diction.
unfortunitly, some of these vowels also carry bad habbits like dipthongs and unnessary movement of articulators.
The (e) vowel helps to pull the tongue forward but, the down side it that it can close the jaw. On the othe hand,
(ɑ). vowel helps to lower the jaw and larynx, but it can also pull the tongue back. The (ɔ). vowel helps to establish
a rounded lip position and is good for blending with others. The (u). vowel helps to keep the sides of a singers
lips more forward and is great to prevent spreading lips and loosing air. The (u) vowel prevents the natural tentancy
to spread the sides of the lips that can be caused by the (e) vowel. The (ɛ) vowelhelps to lift the
cheeks and upper lip to allow sound to hit the hard surface of the teeth to creat a brighter sound. Good diction happens when
the jaw is lowered, the toungue is forward, and the lips are more puckered. This happens because the singers articulators
are closer together and don't need to take the time to keep repositioning for articulation.

Consonants

A consonant is a speech sound that is articulated with all or partial closure of the vocal tract. There aer an array of
consonants a singer has at their disposal. Voice Fricitives (v,th,z) really help loosen the muscles of the articulators through
their vibrations. Unvoiced Fricitives (f,s,sh) really help a singer with support because of the air blockedge created. voice
Plosives (p,t,k) can act like a clapper of a bell that helps project a singers voice and the nasil consonants (m,n,ng) really help to
activate the resonating chambers. Unvoice plosives, or implosives (B,d,g) are very helpful to understand the ratio of support between consonants
and vowels.

Focus

The focus is the deliberate muscular movements that are focused in an area to produce a sound that
can be well heard and understood. Almost every room has a focal point. A central point that all sound
vibrations are naturally drawn to by the acoustics of the room. This is the point in the room that all
sound returns to after bouncing off the surfaces in the room. A focused voice is like a focused laser beam,
it concentrates the voice to the focal point in the room to create more sound and use less air.

OverTones

An overtone is any tone greater than the fundamental frequency of a sound and together are called harmonics.
Harmonic partials, are partials whose frequencies are numerical integer multiples of the fundamental frequency.
The fundamental note being sung in a given room vibrates at a specific Hz causing a secondary vibration in said room.
This secondary vibration is double the Hz of the fundamental frequency of an octave higher and is known as the 1st over tone.
The vibration from the 1nd over tone creates a 3rd over tone that is double the Hz of the 1st overtone making it an octave and a
fifth above the fundamental note being sung. Each over tone being double the Hz of the tone below it.

Formants

Formants are close groupings of overtones in a given room. These close groupings of overtones are created by the
fundamental tone created by each resonating chamber of the singer that amplifies the voice like a bell over any orchestra.
